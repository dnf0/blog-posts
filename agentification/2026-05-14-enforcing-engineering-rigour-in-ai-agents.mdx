# Enforcing Engineering Rigour in AI Agents

*May 14, 2026 • 10 min read • Tags: `ai-workflows`, `orchestration`, `developer-experience`*

<Callout type="info">
**TL;DR:** Deploying raw AI models across an organisation leads to fragmented workflows and chaotic codebases. By adopting the unified `agent-rules` ecosystem—which seamlessly integrates cross-provider standardisation, stateful orchestration, and expert capabilities like `graphify` and `roborev-integration`—I was able to standardise developer experience without bloat and provide a foolproof onboarding path for non-engineers.
</Callout>

I was recently tasked at my current organisation with identifying a curated set of tooling to standardise our developer experience (DevEx) across AI-assisted workflows. The mandate was twofold: enforce engineering rigour without introducing heavy bureaucratic bloat, and provide a frictionless onboarding experience for non-engineering users venturing into terminal-based AI tooling like the Gemini CLI and Claude Code for the first time. 

When I initially observed unguided AI agents operating within our codebases, they acted without context, discipline, or standard operating procedures. The results were wildly unpredictable. An agent might write a brilliant algorithm one moment and execute a destructive, unverified refactor the next. Unconstrained AI is an organisational liability, not an asset. To fix this, I introduced strict workflow orchestration—forcing these models to abandon their erratic autonomy and adopt systematic engineering rigour.

## The Problem: Fragmented Ecosystems and Stateless Agents

My first observation was the severe fragmentation of the AI tooling ecosystem. Different teams were using a disparate array of IDEs and CLI tools—Cursor, Claude Code, and the Gemini CLI. Each tool required its own configuration schema, leading to massive context drift. An architectural rule established in `.cursorrules` was completely ignored by Claude. 

Furthermore, I discovered a critical limitation in relying purely on system prompts: static rules are inherently stateless. I could write a mandate to "always write tests," but in the heat of a complex implementation, an agent would routinely skip verification if it hit a roadblock. I needed a mechanism to force the AI to empirically validate its assumptions before claiming a task was complete.

## Curating the Lean Stack

To solve this, I deliberately avoided heavy, monolithic platforms. Instead, I curated a subset of highly composable tools that, in combination, provided everything needed for a rigorous engineering organisation.

### Unification via agent-rules

To resolve the fragmentation problem, I deployed the `agent-rules` repository to act as the single source of truth. I authored our core engineering mandates in a universal `AGENTS.md` file. The tooling then dynamically compiled this master file into native formats tailored for each provider (e.g., `.cursorrules`, `CLAUDE.md`, `GEMINI.md`). 

This cross-provider generation meant I only had to update a policy once. Regardless of whether an engineer was using Cursor in a Codespace or the Gemini CLI locally, the baseline rules of engagement were universally enforced. 

### Orchestration via Superpowers

Establishing static rules was insufficient; I needed to orchestrate the actual workflow. I integrated `@obra/superpowers` [[1]](#references) to act as an orchestration layer. Superpowers overrides default agent behaviours by forcing them to follow step-by-step methodologies. 

For instance, when a developer asks an agent to build a feature, the `writing-plans` skill forces the agent to stop, explore the repository, and write a test-driven development (TDD) plan before writing a single line of implementation code. By mandating processes like planning, TDD, and systematic debugging, I transformed erratic agents into disciplined, methodical engineers. The orchestrator ensures that agents cannot bypass critical verification steps.

### Dynamic Specialisation via Skills

With robust orchestration in place, I needed a way to inject domain-specific expertise without bloating the global context window. Dumping the documentation for every library we use into `AGENTS.md` was unscalable.

I achieved this by arming our orchestrated workflows with specific expert tools, known as "skills." I bundled crucial internal capabilities directly into the ecosystem, specifically `graphify` [[2]](#references) and `roborev-integration` [[3]](#references). 

These two skills are not just arbitrary add-ons; they are fundamentally complementary to our overarching orchestration strategy. `graphify` forces the agent to map the architectural dependencies of an entire codebase into a clustered knowledge graph before making any structural changes, completely eliminating the "blind refactoring" problem. Following implementation, `roborev-integration` locks the agent into a strict, continuous code review loop, ensuring every line of code is rigorously validated against the original specification before the developer ever reviews a pull request. Together, they form an airtight engineering loop: `graphify` ensures the AI understands the system before it builds, and `roborev` ensures it proves its work before it merges. This approach delivers a highly contextualised, bloat-free intelligence layer that prioritises empirical verification over raw velocity.

## Zero-to-Hero: Onboarding Non-Engineers

Architectural purity is functionally useless if it creates friction for adoption. My second mandate was to ensure that non-engineering users—product managers, technical writers, and analysts—could leverage these tools without facing a steep technical learning curve.

I engineered "zero-to-hero" bootstrap scripts and prebuilt templates within the `agent-rules` ecosystem. With a single shell command, any user could inject this entire cognitive architecture into their local environment. 

This democratisation was the final piece of the puzzle. Non-engineers did not need to understand the intricacies of stateful orchestration, cross-provider generation, or TDD. They simply ran the bootstrap script and immediately inherited a disciplined, highly capable AI assistant that was pre-configured to adhere to the organisation's rigorous standards.

## Conclusion: A Cohesive DevEx

By deploying the unified `agent-rules` ecosystem—which intrinsically bundles stateful orchestration and expert skills directly into its standardisation pipeline—I was able to standardise the developer experience across our entire organisation without introducing unnecessary bloat. I learned that the future of AI engineering is not defined solely by the raw intelligence of the underlying models, but by the disciplined workflows and context management we wrap around them.
